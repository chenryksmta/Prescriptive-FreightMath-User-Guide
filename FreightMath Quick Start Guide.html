Foundation

How the Cost Model Works

Understanding what drives the numbers before you analyze them — the mechanics behind every OR figure in FreightMath.

FreightMath is an Activity-Based Costing (ABC) model. Every dollar from your GL is distributed to individual loads using actual activity drivers — miles, hours under power, and load-specific charges. Understanding how that distribution works — and the special cost treatments for certain operations — makes every OR figure you see more interpretable.

Core OR is the load itself — revenue generated against the cost to move it. But a load doesn't exist in a vacuum. Every load originates somewhere and delivers somewhere, and those markets carry their own freight conditions.

FreightMath captures this with two area-level metrics blended into the final FMOR:

  • IB OR (assigned from origin) — the average profitability of inbound freight in the market the load picks up from. Reflects demand-side health: how efficiently freight flows into that origin market.
  • OB OR (assigned from destination) — the average profitability of outbound freight in the market the load delivers into. Reflects supply-side health: what awaits the driver after delivery.

IB OR and OB OR are not properties of an individual load — they are properties of a market area, calculated from historical performance of all loads that moved through it. A load inherits the conditions of the markets it touches.

Why FMOR Diverges from Core OR A strong Core OR load delivering into a market where outbound freight is scarce and costly will carry a high OB OR — pulling FMOR well above what the load itself earned. Two loads with identical Core OR can have very different network cost depending on which markets they touch.

Two common operating characteristics require specialized cost treatment in FreightMath. Understanding both helps explain why OR on team-driven or temperature-controlled loads may differ from what you'd expect.

Team Driver Premium

Team-operated trucks cost more per mile than single-driver trucks — primarily because of higher driver pay. FreightMath accounts for this by weighting team miles at a premium (typically ~37%) when distributing total mileage cost. The model converts all miles to "effective single miles" before computing a cost-per-mile rate, then applies the premium multiplier to team loads. The result: team loads absorb the cost that reflects their actual operational demand, rather than being charged the same per-mile rate as single-driver freight.

Reefer Fuel Allocation

For temperature-controlled operations, reefer fuel is allocated proportionally by temperature category — not flat per hour. Each category (Dry, Protect from Freeze, Chill, Frozen, Deep Frozen) carries a planning burn rate that reflects the actual thermodynamic demand it places on the refrigeration unit. Dry loads receive a $0 allocation; deep frozen loads absorb significantly more than chill. Total dollars allocated always equal total dollars pulled from the GL — only the distribution across loads changes.

What This Means for Your OR If your operation includes team drivers or temperature-controlled freight, loads in those categories will carry OR figures that reflect their true cost — not a blended average. Frozen loads that look expensive aren't model errors; they reflect what that freight actually costs to run.

MapLedger is the data layer that connects your GL to FreightMath. Every cost line in your chart of accounts is mapped to an allocation method — variable costs tied directly to activity (miles, hours, load count), and overhead spread across the network using a workday-based model.

  • Variable Costs — driver pay, fuel, tolls, reefer fuel — allocated based on the actual activity each load generated.
  • Overhead Costs — insurance, depreciation, admin — spread using the Overhead Workday Model, which distributes fixed cost across the operating days in each period.

This mapping is set up during onboarding and reviewed periodically. The quality of your FreightMath OR figures is directly tied to the accuracy of your MapLedger configuration. If an OR figure looks structurally wrong, MapLedger is the first place to investigate.

The Foundation of Every OR Every OR figure in FreightMath — Core, IB, OB, FM — flows from the MapLedger cost allocation. A clean GL mapping produces trustworthy load-level costs. A miscategorized cost line shows up as systematic OR distortion.

Step One

Quick Triage

Get your bearings in 5 minutes — identify the biggest cost and revenue signals before going deep.

Begin every FreightMath session here. Unfamiliar with a metric? The FreightMath Glossary has definitions for every term used across the platform. Quick Triage surfaces the highest-priority items across your network so you know exactly where to focus your analysis time.

Look for red arrows representing period-over-period degradation and attack those areas first. The dashboard home gives you an immediate visual signal of where performance has moved in the wrong direction — don't scroll past a red arrow without noting it for follow-up.

Pro Tip Red arrows are your to-do list. Every red arrow on the dashboard home is an action item, not just an observation.

Why This Matters High density means you have enough data to act with confidence. High OR means the cost structure isn't working. That combination demands urgency.

Step Two

Baseline Review

Establish your cost structure foundation before drawing any conclusions from variance.

With triage complete, move into Baseline Review to understand the structural story behind your numbers — who's performing, who's costing you, and where the operational gaps live.

This chart can be centered either on your current average FM OR or with an OR of 100 as the center point. I recommend filtering to your AVG OR — it gives you a realistic picture of who's above and below your actual baseline, not an arbitrary threshold.

Read the four quadrants this way:

  • Top Right — Protect these. High revenue, favorable OR. These are your best customers; keep them happy and don't let rates erode.
  • Top Left — Likely backhaul customers. Lower revenue but good OR. If they're not serving a backhaul purpose, pricing may be an issue worth investigating.
  • Bottom Right — Operations issue. High revenue but elevated OR. Most likely a dwell time problem, poor length-of-haul fit, or cost driver inefficiency. Dig in operationally.
  • Bottom Left — Act quickly. Low revenue and high OR. These customers are causing OR pressure. Need pricing or operational attention immediately.
Profitability Matrix Customer OR Quadrant Analysis

Track the FreightMath OR line in the combo chart, looking to see where you perform best. Most carriers have a sweet spot in their length-of-haul range that they should actively lean into when making network decisions.

For LOH bands with elevated FM OR, dwell time or poor rate alignment to that length of haul are the most common culprits — both need to be addressed. Filter down by customer and/or lane to isolate exactly what is driving the elevation in those bands.

Pro Tip Once you find the sweet spot, use it as a filter when evaluating new business. If a bid falls outside your best-performing LOH bands, price accordingly or pass.

Step Three

Trend Exploration

Track movement over time to separate structural problems from noise.

Trend Exploration is where patterns emerge. These three pages collectively answer the question every operator needs answered: what changed, where, and why?

It's time to introduce this powerful page. The Area Analysis tells you everything you need to know about a market — the full picture in one view.

The page breaks into three zones:

  • Top Table Summary stats for the market. Outside of FM OR and Core OR, I focus on Broker % in and out — it tells you immediately how dependent you are on the spot market to fill and exit the area.
  • Center Shows what customers and lanes brought you into and out of that market — filterable by customer type and a variety of time windows.
  • Right Panel Expandable — shows what days of the week you have your challenges, what areas you deadheaded into the market from or bounced out to, along with their corresponding FM OR.

This page is critical to understanding the flow. Use it before making any market-level network or pricing decision.

Pro Tip — Go Down the Rabbit Hole Right-click any customer or lane and drill through to find more details. I actively encourage you to explore — the more you click, the more the market story reveals itself.
Area Analysis Market Flow Broker % Deadhead

This page compares periods of 1, 4, or 12 weeks. The top table shows change in customer bill-to volume — up or down — and how that movement impacted FM OR for each individual bill-to. The bottom table is the same view by lane.

Pro Tip 1 — Find Dangerous Growth Sort by largest growth and look for red FM OR. These are lanes you grew that carried a higher-than-network-average OR — you added volume in the wrong places.
Pro Tip 2 — Find Hidden Losses Sort by most reduced and look for green numbers in the FM OR column. These are lanes that performed well but you did less of — for some reason volume dropped on your best freight. Find out why.
Pro Tip 3 — Broker Lens Filter by bill-to type Broker to see exactly where you booked more or less broker freight by lane. Quickly surfaces over-reliance on spot in specific corridors.
Volume Change FM OR Period Comparison Broker Filter

Similar to Areas of Profit from Step 1, this view shows your best and worst OR by how frequently you handle the freight. Volume tiers have names that matter:

  • Power Lanes — High volume. These define your network identity.
  • Repeat Lanes — Mid volume. The development zone — push these toward Power.
  • Spider Lanes — Low volume. Opportunistic freight — the goal is to move these up or evaluate whether they belong in your network at all.

Ideally you want continual improvement by pushing volume up: Spider Lanes → Repeat Lanes → Power Lanes. That progression is a sign of a healthier, more predictable network.

Immediate Action Clicking the Power Lane / High FM OR number will show you the lanes that are simultaneously high volume and hurting your network. These need to be addressed immediately — they have the most impact of any bucket in the matrix.

Step Four

Operational Refinement

Translate findings into decisions — pricing, dispatch, customer strategy, and network design.

This is where FreightMath analysis converts to action. The tools in this step bring data integrity, revenue clarity, and forward-looking scenario planning together into one final loop.

This section surfaces exceptions in the data. Reviewing the underlying causes of each and correcting them will bring the most clarity and organizational trust to FreightMath over time.

It's also a quick way to see where some of your largest misses are — because sometimes the outliers are indeed real and not data anomalies. Don't assume every exception is a system error; some are genuine operational problems worth investigating.

Build the Process Developing internal processes that prevent data integrity issues goes a long way. A carrier that reviews QC regularly ends up with a cleaner, more reliable model than one that only looks at it when something breaks.

This page takes a deep dive into fuel surcharge, showing how FSC has trended over selected periods. It also allows you to compare across multiple customers simultaneously — making it easy to quickly identify outliers or poor FSC pegs.

The three columns to cross-reference are:

  • FM OR — Overall operating ratio including fuel recovery
  • Core OR — OR with fuel stripped out, isolating your base cost efficiency
  • FSC / Billed Mi — The per-mile fuel surcharge being recovered from this customer
The Critical Question For customers with poor FSC tables, step back and calculate the impact when diesel is high — and when it's low. Does your linehaul rate provide enough base compensation to offset a weak FSC peg at both extremes of the fuel cycle?

FreightMath analysis connects directly to a suite of pricing tools. Each is built on the same cost model and OR methodology, so your analytical work flows naturally into rate decisions.

  • BidRight — Contract bid pricing using your historical FreightMath data. Pulls your actual cost structure into lane-level rate suggestions, with scenario modeling for volume commitments.
  • SpotCheck — Spot rate validation built on your OR thresholds. Quickly evaluate whether a load is priced at or above your cost floor before committing.
  • Dedicated Pricing — For DCC opportunities, two modes are available: Quick Bid for fast lane-level estimates and Full Version for comprehensive dedicated cost modeling including driver count, equipment utilization, and overhead allocation.
Close the Loop The What If Analysis tells you what change to make. BidRight and SpotCheck are how you make it — they translate your OR diagnosis into a rate floor you can defend in a negotiation.
